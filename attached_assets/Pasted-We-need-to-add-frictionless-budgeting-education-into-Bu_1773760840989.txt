We need to add frictionless budgeting education into Budget.schoolstack.ai without slowing users down or forcing them through training.

Objective
Build an in-product “budgeting co-pilot” for beginner and intermediate school leaders. The product should teach just enough finance in context so users can complete their budget confidently. Do not build a webinar flow, long tutorial, or heavy onboarding course.

Design principles
1. Teach in context, not before use.
2. Keep users moving.
3. Explanations must be plain English, never jargon-heavy.
4. Guidance should feel supportive, not remedial.
5. Advanced users should not feel buried in help text.
6. Critical information should not be hidden behind tiny tooltips only.

Features to build

1) Beginner Guidance Mode
Add a simple user preference at first entry into the Budget product:
- “I know school finance”
- “I know some basics”
- “I want extra guidance”

Store this preference in user settings and use it to control how much instructional help appears by default.

Behavior:
- Advanced mode: compact UI, explainers collapsed by default
- Basics mode: key explainers expanded for major sections
- Extra guidance mode: explainers expanded, more nudges, more diagnostics

2) Inline “Explain this” help on every major budgeting concept
For all major cards, metrics, and input sections, add a visible “Explain this” link or icon that opens a side panel or expandable inline card.

Each explainer must include:
- What this means
- Why it matters
- What healthy vs risky looks like
- What to do next

Start with these concepts:
- Revenue
- Enrollment assumptions
- Tuition assumptions
- Grants and fundraising
- Payroll
- Occupancy / rent
- Program expenses
- Operating surplus / deficit
- Cash flow
- Ending cash
- Reserves
- Budget vs actual
- Fixed vs variable costs

Example style:
Operating margin
What this means: This shows whether your school brings in more money than it spends from normal operations.
Why it matters: A negative margin over time can signal that your model is not sustainable.
What healthy vs risky looks like: Small positive margins are generally healthier than repeated losses.
What to do next: Review staffing, rent, and enrollment assumptions first.

3) Micro-lessons triggered by user behavior
Create small contextual coaching cards that appear only when relevant. These should be dismissible, non-modal, and short enough to read in under 45 seconds.

Trigger examples:
- First time entering revenue assumptions
- First time user sees a negative cash month
- First time payroll exceeds a defined threshold of revenue
- First time user confuses budget and cash flow views
- First time assumptions create unrealistic growth patterns

Example cards:
- “Budget vs Cash Flow”
- “What makes enrollment assumptions realistic?”
- “Why timing matters even if your annual budget looks positive”
- “How to think about fixed vs variable costs”

Do not show all micro-lessons at once. Only trigger them contextually.

4) Budget diagnostics engine
Add a rules-based diagnostics panel that scans the user’s budget and surfaces the top issues in plain English.

Label the section:
“What should I fix first?”

Show only the top 3 highest-priority issues.

Each issue should include:
- Headline
- Short explanation
- Why it matters
- Recommended action
- Direct link/button to the relevant section

Example:
Issue: Cash goes negative in November
Why it matters: You may not be able to cover payroll and bills even if your annual budget looks balanced.
Recommended action: Review payment timing, reserves, and large fall expenses.
Action button: “Review cash flow assumptions”

Initial diagnostic rules to include:
- Negative ending cash in any month
- Payroll exceeds recommended threshold relative to revenue
- Enrollment growth jumps too fast
- Tuition collection assumptions are unusually high
- Rent / occupancy costs too high relative to revenue
- Grants make up too much of recurring operating revenue
- Expenses growing faster than revenue
- No reserve cushion
- Large mismatch between annual surplus and monthly cash position

Architect this so we can add more rules later.

5) KPI transparency
For each major KPI, allow users to click “How is this calculated?”

Show:
- Formula
- Inputs used
- Linked assumptions
- Plain-English interpretation

Example:
Ending Cash = Beginning Cash + Cash Inflows - Cash Outflows

The goal is to build trust and teach users how the product thinks.

6) Optional 5-minute primer
Add one optional entry-point called:
“Budgeting basics for school founders”

This should be a short, skimmable experience with 4-6 cards max:
- Revenue
- Expenses
- Budget vs cash flow
- Reserves
- Common budgeting mistakes
- What lenders and funders care about

This should be optional, easy to skip, and accessible later from Help.

Do not make this a required onboarding flow.

7) Section-level coaching language
Throughout the app, revise UX copy so it sounds like a smart finance coach:
- calm
- clear
- non-judgmental
- practical

Avoid language that sounds academic, condescending, or overly technical.

Bad:
“Your assumptions are incorrect.”
Better:
“This assumption may be hard to sustain for a school at your current size.”

UX requirements
- No blocking modals except the initial guidance mode selection, and that should be skippable
- No forced product walkthrough
- No long walls of text
- Explanations must be mobile-responsive and readable
- Use progressive disclosure
- Important concepts should be visible, not buried
- Dismissed coaching cards should stay dismissed unless the underlying issue changes materially

Engineering requirements
- Build this as a reusable coaching/help system, not one-off hardcoded text scattered across the app
- Create a structured content model for explainers and micro-lessons:
  - id
  - title
  - body
  - trigger condition
  - audience level
  - related section
  - dismissible status
- Create a reusable diagnostics engine with configurable thresholds
- Keep thresholds easy to adjust
- Track user interactions so we can later analyze:
  - which explainers are opened
  - which lessons are dismissed
  - which diagnostics are most common
  - whether users who engage with guidance complete budgets at higher rates

Suggested UI components
- ExplainerDrawer
- InlineHelpCard
- DiagnosticPanel
- KpiFormulaModal or Drawer
- BeginnerModeToggle
- MicroLessonCard
- HelpCenter entry for “Budgeting basics”

Suggested rollout order
Phase 1
- Beginner Guidance Mode
- Inline Explain This on core sections
- KPI transparency on top metrics

Phase 2
- Diagnostics panel
- Contextual micro-lessons

Phase 3
- Optional 5-minute primer
- Analytics and refinement

Acceptance criteria
- A beginner can understand key budgeting concepts without leaving the product
- An advanced user can move quickly without too much friction
- The app surfaces problems in plain English
- The UI teaches through use, not through mandatory training
- Guidance feels like a co-pilot, not a class
- The system is reusable and scalable across Budget.schoolstack.ai

Please implement this in a clean, modular way and preserve the SchoolStack brand: warm, professional, modern, supportive, never patronizing.
