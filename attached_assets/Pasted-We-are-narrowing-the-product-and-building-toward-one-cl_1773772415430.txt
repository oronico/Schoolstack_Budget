We are narrowing the product and building toward one clear outcome:

SchoolStack Budget should become the best budgeting and financial model builder for K-12 entrepreneurs in America. It should take a founder who feels overwhelmed by finance, guide them step by step through building a realistic model, help them understand what the numbers mean, show them what to fix, and generate lender-ready outputs they can actually use.

We are not trying to become the full SchoolStack operating system right now.
We are making Budget an exceptional wedge product first.

Core product loop
1. founder enters assumptions
2. product explains what they mean
3. product analyzes viability and risk
4. product identifies the top 3 issues to fix
5. founder revises assumptions
6. product compares scenarios
7. founder generates lender-ready and board-ready outputs

Product principles
- teach in context, not before use
- reduce fear and math avoidance
- plain English first, formulas second
- trust is a product feature
- every major screen should answer: what does this mean, why does it matter, what should I do next
- no unnecessary complexity, no spreadsheet chaos, no jargon-heavy UX

Build plan
We are executing this in 3 epics.

EPIC 1. Alpha hardening and trust layer
Goal: make the model and exports trustworthy enough for alpha users and lender-facing outputs.

Scope:
- centralize financial logic as much as possible
- reduce export-to-export drift
- quarantine or deprecate legacy export paths
- verify escalation / inflation input wiring
- clarify debtIncluded behavior
- add deterministic golden-model regression tests with exact expected outputs
- document monthly cash flow timing assumptions
- preserve and refine KPI formula transparency
- complete Phase 1 coaching stabilization and instrumentation

Definition of done:
- one clear source of truth for core math
- exact regression tests pass for key sample school models
- exports tie out and are consistent
- formulas shown in the UI are trustworthy
- coaching events are captured
- product is defensible for alpha

EPIC 2. Decision engine
Goal: transform Budget from a model builder into a decision-support product.

Scope:
- add a “What should I fix first?” panel after analysis
- rank the top 3 issues in plain English
- each issue includes why it matters and a direct link to the relevant assumption section
- add scenario comparison view
- show what changed between scenarios and what improved or worsened
- make clear distinction between annual profitability and monthly cash stress
- strengthen founder-facing interpretations in the consultant analysis view

Initial decision rules should include:
- negative ending cash in any month
- staffing cost too high relative to revenue
- occupancy cost too high relative to revenue
- overly aggressive enrollment growth
- unrealistic collection assumptions
- weak reserves
- heavy reliance on grants for recurring operations
- poor DSCR or weak debt coverage

Definition of done:
- a founder can finish a model and immediately understand the top risks
- users can compare two scenarios and see the impact clearly
- the product recommends actions, not just metrics

EPIC 3. Lender-ready output productization
Goal: make reports and outputs feel like a real underwriting and board communication product, not just exports.

Scope:
- separate lender-ready packet from board-ready packet
- lender packet should include executive summary, assumptions snapshot, 3-5 year forecast, monthly cash flow, DSCR/reserves, key risks and mitigants, and supporting exhibits
- board packet should be simpler, more visual, and decision-oriented
- improve narrative summaries generated from the model
- ensure the product “shows its work” with linked assumptions and formulas where needed

Definition of done:
- output looks professional and coherent
- lender packet feels underwriting-aware
- board packet feels founder-friendly and strategic
- exports tell a financial story, not just dump numbers

Not in scope right now
- full SIS
- CRM
- broad facilities OS expansion
- messaging automation
- benchmark engine
- heavy integrations beyond what is required for the wedge
- broad redesign of the whole product

Working style
- do one epic at a time
- no scope creep
- every epic needs acceptance criteria, QA notes, and a short findings memo
- propose architecture before major refactors
- preserve modularity and avoid duplicate math logic

Please start by breaking Epic 1 into 3-5 tightly scoped implementation tickets in the right order, with dependencies, acceptance criteria, and suggested files.
